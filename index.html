The Problem Underneath

Strategy management isn't the problem.
Scarce judgement is.

Anyone can write a strategy. What organisations can't do is afford enough experienced judgement, applied continuously, to make it real — so the thinking happens in bursts, the execution leaks between meetings, and the people accountable find out what went wrong after it has. The same underlying problem, wearing four different faces:

Consulting Firms & Their End Clients

Clients buy outcomes. The model sells documents.

End clients don't want a deck — they want the turnaround to happen, the market entered, the deal done right. But the leverage model burns most billable hours on junior research and slide production, and when the engagement ends, the judgement leaves with the team. Firms feel it as margin pressure, fee resistance and renewals that hinge on one partner's heroics.

Strategy Offices of Large Corporates

A mandate wider than the team

A handful of people accountable for strategic initiatives across a dozen business units. The capacity goes into chasing status decks and assembling reviews — not into the analysis and intervention the mandate actually calls for. Insight arrives at the quarterly review, which is precisely too late to change the outcome.

High-Growth Businesses

Complexity compounds faster than the leadership team

Every stage of growth multiplies the decisions — new markets, pricing, capacity, people, capital. The honest problem isn't writing the annual plan; it's that growing successfully requires experienced judgement the business can't yet afford to hire or engage. So the biggest bets of the company's life run on instinct and a spreadsheet.

Independent Consultants

All the judgement, none of the machine

The expertise is real — that's why clients hire you. But behind every big-firm consultant stands research staff, knowledge systems and production support. Solo, you are all of those people at midnight. Capacity caps the practice, and prep eats the hours clients actually pay for.
